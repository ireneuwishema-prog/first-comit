imigenzo n’imico sacred customs & living traditions

Intore

The royal dancers, once warriors who embodied bravery. Today they perform the majestic ‘dance of heroes’ with cowry shells and spears.

amayora / drumming

Imigongo

Unique geometric cow-dung art, originating from the ancient kingdom of Gisaka. Curves and spirals in natural earth tones.

ubukorikori / craft

Umuganda

Community work on the last Saturday of every month. A tradition of cooperation that rebuilt the nation.

ubwumvikane / solidarity

Inyambo

The long-horned Ankole cows, praised in song by royal bards. Their horns reflect the majesty of the hills.

ibicurarangwe / poetry

Agaseke

Peace basket weaving — intricate patterns, symbols of gifts and reconciliation. Woven by women with timeless skill.

ubuhumbure / weaving

Ibyivugo

Self-praise poetry recited by heroes. A powerful oral tradition that boosts courage and preserves lineage.

ubusizi / poetry

Ingoma — the heart beat

The royal drum ‘Kalinga’ is only beaten at enthronement. Drums like Isakara, Igihumurizo accompany the Intore dancers, echoing from the hills.

✧ Drums are sacred — they represent the king’s power and the nation’s unity ✧

umuhogo n'umwumbwe gastronomy & artisanal heritage

Ubushera

Sweet millet & sorghum drink, traditionally offered to guests.

Umutsima

Cassava & maize loaf, often paired with beans or sauce.

Imigongo

Natural pigments: red (clay), white (kaolin), black (banana ashes).

Ubwenge

Wisdom passed through proverbs (imigani) & storytelling.

“U Rwanda rwacu, rugari kandi rwiza. Umuco wacu niwo muryango w’umuryango”
(Our Rwanda, vast and beautiful. Our culture is the family of families.)
— traditional saying

Gutwikurura

Traditional marriage ceremony where the groom offers a cow (inkwano) to the bride’s family. Symbol of gratitude and union.

Umuganura

Thanksgiving harvest festival, the first fruits are offered to the king — a time of joy and unity.

Kwita Izina

Naming ceremony for newborns, akin to the famous gorilla naming, but rooted in family lineage.
